\section{Data Sources}
\label{sec:data-sources}
Technical recruitment often requires multiple data sources to be provided to evaluate candidates beyond traditional CVs, but are often rarely used. These sources have the potential to provide insights into the extensive professional history, technical competences, behavioural tendencies as well as real-world coding practices. Effective multi-sources screening will require a deeper understanding on the strengths and limitations of these data channels.

\subsection{Commonly Provided data sources}
The following data sources are commonly provided by candidates during technical recruitment processes:

\subsubsection*{Curriculum Vitae}
A CV remains the primary go-to document for recruitment pipelines, for technical roles it is especially important, allowing recruiters to quickly compare candidates without the need to commit to an extensive interview cycle for every candidate. 
A typical technical CV will include a candidate's name, education, employment history, projects and technical skills. 
Despite the amount of key data provided, research often shows that CVs do not provide enough information and often contains unreliable representations due to embellishment and overestimation \cite{birkeland2006_applicant_faking_behavior,highhouse2008stubborn}. 
For this very reason, organisations increasingly supplement CVs with behavioural and performance-based evidence.

\subsubsection*{GitHub} 
GitHub is a central platform for hosting projects and version-controlled repositories. 
For developers, it is arguably the richest source of work-sample evidence, capturing coding style, commit history, collaboration practices, documentation depth, and long-term engagement patterns. 
Several hiring reports note that many candidates include GitHub links in applications \cite{linkedin2023_fos,stackoverflow2023}. However, review of these repositories by recruiters is often inconsistent, and may be limited to high-level elements such as README files rather than deeper technical behaviour. 
Despite this, GitHub remains a highly valuable source of work-sample evidence.

\subsubsection*{LinkedIn} LinkedIn functions as a professional identity platform, it allows users to expand upon their projects, experiences and education, offering significantly more information that what may be provided on a CV. 
It also functions as a professional social media platform, where users can share their opinions on different topics. 
As self reported from LinkedIn, around 90\% of recruiters use LinkedIn as part of candidate evaluation, especially during early-stage screening \cite{linkedin2023_fos}. 
However, it is important to note that this is self-reported by LinkedIn themselves, so this statistic may be overinflated and subject to bias.

\subsection{Other data sources}
There are also other data sources that, while not often provided, can offer valuable insights into a candidate's suitability for a technical role. These include:


\subsubsection*{Leetcode} LeetCode is one of the most widely used platforms for practicing algorithmic problems that often appear in technical interviews and online assessments. 
Sources show that 76\% of companies use coding challenges in their hiring process, and these challenges may often resemble LeetCode problems \cite{hackerrank2023skills}. 
Because of this, a candidate's activity on coding platforms can reveal problem-solving patterns, topic strengths, and recent engagement. It may also highlight areas where candidates are weaker, helping recruiters assess adaptability to new or unfamiliar problems.

\subsubsection*{HackerRank} HackerRank is quite similar to LeetCode, in terms of it being a platform for coding challenges. 
It is also widely used as a platform to host online technical tests, setting it apart from its competitor. 
Many companies, especially those hiring at scale rely on HackerRank for standardised assessments, making the platform an interesting data source for understanding a candidate's tested technical ability.

\subsubsection*{Stack Overflow} Stack Overflow is a Q\&A platform for software engineering knowledge exchange. 
Contributions on Stack Overflow can provide insight into a candidate's communication style, community engagement, and areas of expertise. 
Prior research shows that contributions on such platforms can correlate with professional experience and also domain specialisation \cite{vasilescu2013}.

\subsubsection*{Social Media} During background checks, organisations may review social flags, though this is often to identify red flags rather than evaluate technical competence. 
Research shows that around 54\% of employers perform some form of social media screening. 
Due to the practice being controversial due to privacy, bias and fairness concerns, MERIT will not consider non-professional social media as a supplementary data source.
Note that professional social media such as LinkedIn is still considered.

\subsection*{Relevance to MERIT}
These diverse data sources show the need for a screening system that can integrate, interpret and contextualise various forms of candidate data. 
MERIT must be designed with extensibility and multimodal capability in mind. 
By supporting current sources (CVs, GitHub, LinkedIn, coding platforms) and enabling the integration of future sources, MERIT can provide rich, reliable insights into candidate abilities and also reduce reliance on superficial, incomplete, and biased assessments.
