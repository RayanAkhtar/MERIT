\section{Automated Screening}

Automated screening systems (commonly referred to as Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS)), are used by many organisations to filter, rank and manage large volumes of candidates.
Among large firms, usage is common, with data from Fortune 500 companies showing that nearly all (approx. 98\%) use an ATS as part of their hiring pipeline \cite{jobscan2022}.
While ATS adoption rates outside of the largest firms are not as certain, these systems remain a core part of scaling the recruitment pipeline in high-volume hiring environments.
\\\\
While ATS implementations differ significantly, they can be categorised into one of three types: Rule-Based, Keyword-Matching, and AI-Assisted.

\subsection{Rule-Based ATS}
These forms of ATS were the first to be introduced, and often the simplest.
They work by parsing resumes into structured fields, such as skills, education, job titles and dates.
From here, explicit, pre-defined rules are applied in order to screen candidates.
Recruiters often configure keyword lists, required skill thresholds, boolean queries and knockout conditions (e.g., minimum degree requirements and years of experience).
\\\\
Rule-based systems are relied upon for their consistency and predictability, since decision logic is transparent and fully deterministic.
When rules are clearly defined, these systems are able to reduce the variance across evaluators and avoid subjective bias present in individual decisions \cite{bogen2018}.
\\\\
However, these systems face well-known limitations, especially with standardisation.
These systems will struggle with non-standard CV formats, leading to parsing errors that disproportionately impact early-career applicants as well as those from non-traditional backgrounds \cite{vanesch2019}.
Along with this, these systems are vulnerable to keyword-gaming, a practice where applicants strategically insert repeated or irrelevant terms to artificially inflate their match scores \cite{marr2019}.
These hurdles are then compounded by a failure to recognise unconventional experience, performing poorly when relevant skills are described in non-traditional ways, or demonstrated through alternative means such as open-source contributions or bootcamp projects.


\subsection{Keyword-Matching ATS}
This version of ATS is a middle ground between the previously mentioned rule-based approach, and the upcoming AI-driven version.
This form still relied on keyword matching, but also incorporates better NLP-based parsing to recognise linguistic variations and identify skills even when phrased differently (e.g., “managed projects” → “project management”).
These systems offer limited semantic understanding, able to recognise that related job titles or synonyms carry similar meanings.
\\\\
Their strengths are mainly that there is increased flexibility and better extraction accuracy.
There is also the improved ability in matching candidates who used varied phrasing, and also that there is more transparency than the AI-driven systems, since much of the logic is still rule-based.
\\\\
Despite the improvements, many issues still persist, especially with interpretation.
These systems usually misinterpret contextual details as well as ambiguous language within a resume.
The lack of sophistication also may lead to them overlooking crucial nuances such as depth of a skill, the quality of a candidate’s experience, as well as specific evidence of measurable impact.
Due to this, the limitations often result in misclassification, estimating that approximately 75\% of CVs are filtered out before reaching a human recruiter, often incorrectly identifying qualified candidates as unsuitable matches \cite{kelly2021}.

\subsection{AI-Assisted ATS}
This form of ATS leverage machine learning models that have been trained on historical hiring data to predict candidate-job fit.
Unlike the previous two groups AI-assisted ATS don't rely on keyword matching and  instead infer relationships between skills, roles, qualifications and past hiring decisions.
\\\\
AI-Assisted ATS usually use semantic search and vector-based skill matchmaking to understand the conceptual intent behind a resume, alongside skill inference drawn from conceptual data.
They also employ predictive scoring models that learn from recruiter interactions, and utilise pattern recognition to identify hidden, non-obvious candidate strengths that may otherwise go unnoticed.
\\\\
Their strengths include higher matchmaking accuracy, as well as the ability to detect transferable skills, not explicitly written on CVs. They also handle non-traditional career paths more effectively than rule-based systems.
\\\\
However, there are also many challenges faced by AI-Assisted ATS systems too.
Firstly, algorithmic bias is a notable issue where models inherit and reproduce historical patterns of gender, ethnic or institutional preference found in past hiring data \cite{raghavan2020,bogen2018}.
This issue is made worse with feedback loops, as systems that consistently prioritise specific candidate profiles may continue to further these traits even as a company’s strategic needs change \cite{holstein2019}.
\\\\
Furthermore, the “black box” nature of these systems lead to low transparency, as there is often little explanation for ranking and rejecting decisions, undermining both the candidate trust as well as compliance with fair-hiring guidelines.
Another significant issue lies in over-generalisation, where these models are often trained on broad datasets, prioritising generic markers over role-specific expertise.
In a similar fashion, domain drift also plays a role, where the system continues to optimise for outdated historical traits despite a companies' technological or strategic shifts.
\\\\
It is critical to note that almost all ATS platforms operate exclusively on CV and application data, limiting their ability to capture verified evidence of a candidate's true capabilities.
