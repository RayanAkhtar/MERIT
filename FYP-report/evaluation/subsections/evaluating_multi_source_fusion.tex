This section evaluates both the mathematical and empirical validity of MERIT's multi-source model under \textbf{RQ2}.
Montandon et al.~\cite{montandon2021_roles} treat GitHub as a supplementary hiring signal rather than a sole criterion
for a candidate's skills. MERIT extends that principle by fusing CV claims with corroborating or contradicting repository evidence.
CV embellishment and self-report bias motivate favouring demonstrated over claimed signals~\cite{henle2019_resume_fraud,dunning2004}.
To ensure a rigorous evaluation, we proceed bottom-up: skill decay sensitivity, Bayesian conflict resolution, algorithmic ablation 
against literature-motivated ATS baselines, and a multi-source data ablation.
We run four scoring engines against the same candidate pool and JD: a \textbf{Traditional ATS} baseline (keyword-based CV screening),
a \textbf{Modern AI ATS} baseline (semantic CV screening), MERIT with only CV signals, and MERIT with all sources.
When referring to ``all'' sources, we specifically refer to the integration of GitHub and LinkedIn signals.
With the same engine setup and candidate pool, we will then proceed with a data ablation study, where we will point out the success and failure cases of the model.
The labels \textbf{Traditional ATS} and \textbf{Modern AI ATS} denote in-repository CV screening baselines that replicate scoring logic typical of rule-based and AI-assisted Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS), they are not proprietary commercial products. MERIT is evaluated as a separate multimodal screening system.

These studies assume that candidate CVs were successfully extracted without error. These evaluations run on emulated, 
synthetic candidate JSON profiles created to systematically test specific edge cases, bypassing the PDF extraction layer 
of MERIT (Stage 1). This allows us to isolate the multi-source Bayesian fusion and other scoring engines from any parsing noise.
By assuming perfect extraction, we establish a clean mathematical baseline so we can verify that the scoring logic, temporal decay
and conflict resolution can be demonstrated and evaluated as intended, rather than being subject to the varied errors of PDF parsing.
Validity implications are in Section~\ref{subsec:internal-validity}.

\subsection{Study 08: Algorithmic Sensitivity and Skill Decay}
\label{subsec:study-08-skill-decay}

Prior research on skill retention shows that unused workplace skills deteriorate over time~\cite{arthur1998}.
Study~08 tests whether MERIT's decay parameter produces sensible rank separation between actively used and stale skills.
This does not calibrate $\lambda$ against Arthur et al.'s effect sizes.
All humans are prone to skill degradation over time. For a technical role, if you are not frequently working with a
specific technology, your expertise will naturally get ``rusty'' the longer you are removed from it. For candidates, we
consider the technical skill of a candidate $S(t)$ to be a function of time $t$ (in years) since the skill was
last actively practised.
\begin{equation}
    S(t) = S_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t}
\end{equation}
where $S_0$ is the initial unweighted skill score extracted by the ingestion engine, and $\lambda$ is the
skill decay constant. In this implementation, $\lambda$ is set to $\approx 0.25$, corresponding
to a skill half-life of exactly $t_{1/2} = 2.77$ years, as described in Section~\ref{subsec:skill-decay-modelling}.

To evaluate two candidates to compare the effects of this model, both candidates have the exact same CV history,
with the only differing factor being the active time of usage for these skills (evaluated against a study baseline year of 2024):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Candidate A (Active Pro):} Demonstrates active, continuous Go and Kubernetes usage from
          2022 to 2024 ($t = 0$).
    \item \textbf{Candidate B (Stale Pro):} Possesses identical technical depth in Go and Kubernetes,
          but has not actively worked with either technology since their last recorded project in 2018 ($t = 6$ years).
\end{itemize}

Applying the temporal decay formula to Candidate B's Go skill will yield the following:
\begin{equation}
    S(6) = S_0 \cdot e^{-0.25 \times 6} = S_0 \cdot e^{-1.5} \approx 0.223 \cdot S_0
\end{equation}

This means that Candidate B's Go skill score is only 22.3\% of what it would be if they were actively working with Go.
It is likely that within 6 years, the candidate has forgotten some syntax of the language,
but also the common libraries and frameworks used within it. If this candidate were to be selected for the role,
they would likely require some upskilling in order to get up to speed with the current best practices for
Go, and would experience a ramp-up period in the initial months.





\subsection{Study 12: Bayesian Conflict Resolution}
\label{subsec:study-12-bayesian-conflict-resolution}

The reasoning for MERIT's multi-source fusion layer is to be able to reward candidates who are able to demonstrate their skills 
with publicly available evidence. This is particularly important for extensible metrics, where said metric is tied directly
to the job description that it was derived from. We believe it to be essential to reward candidates who can prove that they are
fit for the role, rather than just claim to be fit for it. In this study, we verify the Beta-Bernoulli conjugate updating 
implementation \cite{gelman2013bayesian} with two controlled hypothetical profiles on the same extensible metric, motivated by the need to down-weight self-reported CV claims that personnel research shows are frequently embellished~\cite{henle2019_resume_fraud,dunning2004}.
The first
represents a claims-only candidate, while the second represents a demonstration-only candidate. Both candidates achieve the 
maximum score available for the signals that correspond to the category of evidence (claimed or demonstrated).

In this walkthrough, the maximum signal that signals which are claims-only can achieve is $0.8$, while the maximum signal for demonstration-only signals
is uncapped at $1.0$. We start from priors $\alpha_0 = \beta_0 = 0.1$, and the step-by-step walkthrough is given in Table~\ref{tab:bayesian-walkthrough}.

At the GitHub step (Table~\ref{tab:bayesian-walkthrough}, row~2, Figure~\ref{fig:bayesian-fusion-convergence}), the two profiles diverge sharply. The demonstration-only candidate reaches $0.624$, while the claims-only candidate falls to $0.314$, a gap of 31 percentage points. This reflects the higher source confidence assigned to demonstrated GitHub evidence ($c_{\mathrm{GH}} = 0.9$), and the fact that a zero GitHub signal actively contradicts the CV claim rather than leaving it unchallenged.

For the claims-only profile, a capped CV assertion ($s_1 = 0.8$, $c_1 = 0.5$) initially lifts the score from $0.500$ to $0.711$. This is
broadly the sort of outcome a CV-only screening pipeline might produce. After GitHub contradicts the claim, the score drops to $0.314$, and a
further LinkedIn endorsement ($s_3 = 0.8$, $c_3 = 0.2$) only recovers it to $0.369$. Additional self-report cannot substitute for missing
public work samples.

The demonstration-only profile follows the reverse pattern. An empty CV ($s_{\mathrm{CV}} = 0$) briefly pulls the score down to $0.148$
before GitHub ingestion, after which strong demonstrated evidence lifts it to $0.624$ and the final fused score settles at $0.555$. When we
enable multi-source fusion in this way, MERIT rewards verifiable work and does not treat self-report as sufficient when repositories
contradict it. This bias towards demonstrated evidence is intentional. Self-reported CVs are embellished frequently, and a system that
treated claimed and demonstrated signals equally would be straightforward to game. MERIT is therefore designed to favour candidates who
can back up their skills with public work, while still recording uncertainty (rather than a hard rejection) when a candidate has no
demonstrated evidence to show.

The results demonstrate how the layering of scoring configurations systematically neutralises adversarial manipulation
and corrects for temporal or formatting biases. In the Traditional ATS engine (which relies entirely on raw CV keyword
frequency), rankings are dominated by high-tenure profiles and adversarial profiles such as Buzz Ward (\#2), who
successfully exploits keyword-density screening. Under the semantic Modern AI ATS, cosine similarity correctly
evaluates conceptual alignment (elevating Alex Rivers to \#1 and deprioritising Buzz Ward to \#5), yet remains highly
vulnerable to unverified assertions (incorrectly elevating Carl Corp to \#2 despite a total lack of verifiable
professional footprint).

Read against prior work, the Traditional ATS baseline instantiates the keyword-matching category reviewed in Section~\ref{sec:automated-screening}; the Modern AI ATS baseline implements semantic CV--JD similarity with SBERT~\cite{reimers2019sentence}, the same embedding family used in many AI-assisted matchers, whereas Gugnani and Misra~\cite{gugnani2020} train corpus-specific document embeddings with fixed scoring coefficients rather than this baseline architecture.
Montandon et al.~\cite{montandon2021_roles} infer coarse technical roles from GitHub metadata; MERIT (Full) instead fuses claim-level CV signals with corroborating or contradicting repository evidence rather than substituting a role classifier.

For the CV-Only baseline, the introduction of the keyword-stuffing audit penalises Buzz Ward's stuffed CV, but also
highlights a critical structural limitation: without secondary evidence, screening models struggle to differentiate
between genuine high-density expertise and adversarial optimisation, occasionally penalising valid CVs. 
When we enable the full capabilities of MERIT, we are able to resolve these issues through Bayesian Evidence Fusion (BEF). 
This allows the system to verify or challenge claims made by the candidate, further mitigating the issue of adversarial optimisation
unless they have the demonstrated evidence to back it up.

Crucially, while analysing ordinal rank displacements highlights the recruiter's sorting experience, evaluating the
cardinal score adjustments ($\Delta$) between the CV-Only baseline and the Full MERIT multi-source configuration reveals
the true mechanistic sensitivity of the Bayesian fusion engine. 
Since ordinal rankings only measure the relative position of one candidate to another, we end up disregarding the size of the 
shift in score. For instance, Sam Old's rank shift is modest (+2 rank displacement, moving from \#7 to \#5),
yet his cardinal score rises by $+13.8\%$, representing a high-conviction verification of his inactive competencies that was
previously penalised by single-source temporal decay. Similarly, Jordan Smith's transition shows an extraordinary cardinal
leap of $+53.8\%$ (from $10.5\%$ to $64.3\%$), mathematically capturing the breakthrough potential of a junior candidate whose
sparse resume is validated by elite open-source contributions.

Conversely, the fusion engine successfully limits or penalises unverified or adversarial claims: Buzz Ward's stuffed CV
receives a negligible cardinal shift of $+0.7\%$, while Vince Vault experiences a negative cardinal shift of $-12.6\%$
($33.2\% \to 20.6\%$), representing the heavy evidence conflict penalty applied under validation failures. The specific
data-source mechanics driving these individual shifts are detailed in Section~\ref{subsec:study-01b-data-source-ablation}.
Figure~\ref{fig:rank-displacement-bump-chart} (Appendix~\ref{appendix:rank_displacement})
traces the rank trajectories of all ten personas (rank~1 = best fit for the JD), including the cases discussed above.

\subsection{Study 01B: Multi-Source Data Ablation}
\label{subsec:study-01b-data-source-ablation}

This study will cover the critical trade-offs present in a system that doesn't just follow claimed evidence but also takes
into account demonstrated evidence. More specifically, we will see cases where it is beneficial to the system to consider 
multiple sources of evidence, as well as cases where it can penalise candidates unfairly due to the accessibility of their data.

\subsubsection{Systemic Success: The Discovery of Jordan Smith}

The most significant success of multi-source integration lies in Jordan Smith, a junior developer with only 1 year of
formal experience, whose sparse resume is automatically discarded by both traditional and modern semantic ATS (ranking
\#10 and \#9 respectively) due to low tenure.
What is not mentioned on Jordan's CV, however, is his extensive work that is publicly available on his GitHub account, demonstrating
significant technical expertise in both Java and TypeScript, two key technologies in the JD for this study.

Once we account for both LinkedIn and GitHub signals in MERIT (Full), we see that Jordan rises from Rank \#10 to Rank \#2. We are able
to see that his proficiencies in both Java and TypeScript align well with the requirements of the JD.
Jordan has the most demonstrated evidence among the candidate batch, and MERIT (Full) is the only engine that is able to discover this,
and reward him accordingly. By shifting the evaluation paradigm from unverified skills towards verified demonstrable evidence, 
we see that MERIT is able to prevent valid candidates from being lost in the traditional ``Resume Black Hole'' despite being a 
candidate that is quite fit for the role.

\subsubsection{Systemic Limitation: The ``Proprietary Data Gap'' for Carl Corp}
However, the introduction of multi-source evaluation does come with its own set of trade-offs.
The fatal weakness of MERIT's multi-source evaluation is that it is dependent on the accessibility of a candidate's data.
For example, let's consider Carl Corp, a candidate whose data on GitHub is essentially empty, since most of his commits are towards
private repositories. As a result, he scores low for GitHub signals, and is unable to demonstrate his expertise in the programming languages
required by the JD. This results in a fall from Rank \#2 to Rank \#10 between the Modern AI ATS and Full MERIT evaluations.
This ``Proprietary Data Gap'' is a critical limitation of MERIT that will significantly, and unfairly, hinder candidates that are not able
to share their evidence of code, and will unfairly advantage candidates who have less experience but are able to share their code.
This represents a key trade-off in the design of MERIT. While we are now able to avoid some forms of bias due to the multi-source nature
of the system, we are now introducing a new form of bias based on the accessibility of a candidate's data. This raises the question of
whether the benefits of multi-source evaluation outweigh the costs of introducing this new form of bias.


\subsubsection{Integrity Resolution}
\label{subsubsec:identity-mismatch-resolution}

Now that MERIT considers multiple sources of data, it opens up the possibility of candidates trying to game the system
by including false sources of evidence. This will be referred to as \textbf{Identity Squatting}. We will evaluate the effectiveness
of MERIT's integrity resolution mechanisms in detecting and penalising such candidates.

Vince Vault is a candidate who claims a strong 10 years of experience as a Lead Software Architect on his CV, and presents himself as a
Java expert. This will provide an interesting case study into the effectiveness of MERIT's integrity resolution mechanisms.
Here, Vince boasts excessive skills, scoring exceptionally well in a Traditional ATS (\textbf{Rank \#1}) and Modern AI ATS
(\textbf{Rank \#4}). However, it could be the case that Vince is actually experienced in C\#, and upon hearing about Java and C\# being
similar programming languages, he decides to try and game the system by applying to this role as a ``Java expert''.

To prevent GitHub from identifying his proficiencies in C\# and not Java, Vince instead looks for an opportunity to find a candidate who
demonstrates proficiencies in Java, and instead provides a link to someone else's GitHub profile. This is a serious case of identity
squatting and will be used to test the robustness of MERIT's integrity resolution mechanisms. Due to the severe nature of this act of
fraud, MERIT's Integrity Layer applies a flat veto penalty, deducting exactly 20 percentage points ($0.20$ absolute score reduction) from
his score, regardless of the evidence presented by the other sources. The result of this absolute penalisation is highly significant:
Vince's CV-Only score is $33.2\%$, and although the squatted GitHub profile temporarily inflates his raw average to $40.6\%$, the application
of the $20\%$ veto drags his final score to $20.6\%$, dropping his rank from Rank \#4 to Rank \#9. This ensures that, on the backend side,
MERIT is successfully able to identify and penalise candidates who try to game the system.

It is important to note that this is a rare edge case, it is not very common for candidates to engage in this type of fraud.
However, it is important to consider this edge case in the design of MERIT, as it is a potential threat to the integrity
of the system.
