Technical recruitment faces the challenge of growing applicant volumes. The result of this is cognitively
demanding CV reviews, as well as the use of automated screening tools that are often rigid, opaque and 
dependent on the CV as the sole source of information. This paper presents \textbf{M.E.R.I.T}
(Multi-source Employment Ranking \& Insight Tool), referred to as MERIT throughout the rest of the report.
MERIT is a prototype screening pipeline that combines the curriculum vitae (CV) and job description (JD) 
ingestion with evidence from GitHub and LinkedIn, and exposes candidate rankings through glass-box explanations.

MERIT's design is split into three core concepts, tying into the three stages of a screening pipeline.
The first core concept is \emph{extensible} metric extraction, which aims to 
allow recruiters to define their own metrics based on the job description.
The second core concept is \emph{multi-source} fusion, which aims to combine 
evidence from multiple sources to provide a detailed view of a candidate.
The third core concept is \emph{explainable} scoring, which aims to give a more 
transparent and interpretable explanation for the candidate's ranking.

The results demonstrate that multi-source fusion has the capacity to recover qualifies candidates who may
have been overlooked by CV-only baselines when public work samples strongly support claimed skills. This is evidenced
by the highest observed expert rank correlation in one high-discrimination cohort (Study~07, $n=10$, Spearman $\rho = 0.697$, $p < 0.05$).
Results also point towards the effectiveness of explainability, with a formative glass-box pilot ($N=5$) indicating recruiters overturn
misleading shortlists when weak evidence or dishonest behaviour is revealed. However, these achievements do not
come without limitations. Due to the unstructured nature of CVs and JDs, we are subject to parsing errors and noise.
We also observe how multi-source fusion often penalises empty data sources, and may also deviate significantly
from expert orderings under adversarial source conflict.

Therefore, MERIT is best characterised as an \textbf{auditable screening assistant} 
for human-in-the-loop review, rather than a fairness-certified or autonomous hiring authority.
We document both the successes and the failures in in order to support future work towards various research questions.
