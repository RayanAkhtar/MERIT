\subsection{Ethical AI \& Hiring}
\label{sec:ethical-ai-hiring}

As we have seen in previous sections, automated screening tools can reproduce historical bias, hide candidate outcomes, and encourage
candidates to game the system \cite{bogen2018,raghavan2020,fabris2025fairness}. Most ATS products are described as black boxes, meaning that
there is little transparency into the decision-making process. MERIT, on the other hand, shows why a score was produced. The open question,
however, is whether recruiters use this transparency to ensure fairness, or only to defend a decision they had already intended to make.

In Study~14, we observed that the visibility of the Glass-Box breakdown impacted behaviour. Recruiters overturned shortlists when evidence
of keyword stuffing and identity squatting was spotted. In the case of identity squatting, recruiters would reject candidates not on the basis
of their skills, but rather on evidence of dishonesty. We must also note that we cannot be certain that recruiters would have overturned
those shortlists without the Glass-Box breakdown. As stated in Study~15, match scores are intentionally provided in Blind Mode, however this
could introduce some bias. Similarly, the inclusion of tier labels for academic institutions could still introduce bias.

The Integrity Engine aided in blocking most simple cheats, as evident in Study~06, though clever gaming of activity metrics and rare
identity-squat bypasses are still possible. Study~15 was also informative on the effectiveness of Blind Mode in reducing bias. We were able
to observe that unfair biases were mitigated through the design choices made in MERIT. This suggests a fairer first look, but not a proof of
fairness across gender or ethnicity. Note that Bertrand and Mullainathan's field experiments documented name-based discrimination in hiring
callbacks \cite{bertrand2004}, while Study~15 did not test those protected characteristics directly.

MERIT's evidence-first approach means the system is more biased towards candidates who have public work samples, such as GitHub.
This is evident in Study~01B, where Jordan Smith is usually penalised by CV-only systems but uplifted when GitHub is included.
However, this also leads to cases such as Carl Corp, where proprietary data is penalised. In a system where we reward demonstrated evidence,
we may penalise candidates who have no public work samples. Section~\ref{sec:societal-impact} summarises the main risks and mitigations.
