\section{Data Privacy \& GDPR Compliance}
\label{sec:data-privacy-gdpr}

If MERIT is to be deployed and used for real hiring in the UK, it would fall under the data-protection law (UK GDPR) \cite{uk_dpa_2018}.
MERIT stores sensitive PII such as names, contact details and CV content (which in itself contains PII).
Alongside this, it stores LinkedIn content, GitHub activity, which could be considered personal data.
Alongside this, we also store generated outputs such as the match score, Shapley breakdowns and fraud flags.
In production, the employer using this system would be legally responsible for this data, rather than the author of MERIT themself.
Hiring teams usually rely on a contract law, or some form of business need to process applications, here, candidates must be asked for 
consent to process their data \cite{ico_employment_2023}.

MERIT also stores, and displays data such as profile pictures and university names, which may be considered personal data. While this
may be masked in Blind Mode, it is still being stored in the backend database. This data is likely to hint at background characteristics,
which employers must not discriminate on. To encourage this behaviour, Blind Mode is set to be enabled by default, and it is the recruiter's
own choice if they wish to disable it. If MERIT is to be used in the hiring process, employers should publish a clear privacy notice, only 
collecting the data that is necessary for the hiring pprocess, encrypt data and also have a deletion date, and also sign agreements with
third-party hosts. Data gathered for this process should not be reused elsewhere without a separate legal basis.

Article~22 of the GDPR limits fully automated rejections unless appropriate safeguards exist \cite{eu_gdpr_2016}.
It is for this reason that MERIT is designed to be a decision-support tool as opposed to an autonomous screening tool.
A recruiter should still review outcomes (Section~\ref{sec:fairness-computational-rigour}), 
with the option to challenge a score and a record when humans override the ranking. Public GitHub and LinkedIn pages still fall under
the GDPR as personal data, especially since these profiles often store full names, email addresses, phone numbers and other contact details.

One other key consideration is in the scraping of LinkedIn profiles. We currently use the Apify actor \texttt{harvestapi/linkedin-profile-scraper} 
to scrape LinkedIn profiles. This is a third-party service that fetches profiles when given a URL. While this is a convenient way to scrape LinkedIn 
profiles, it is not a compliant way to do so. In production, a system should use the official LinkedIn API, or licensed data feeds, rather than 
Apify-style collection. We have also limited the ingestion of LinkedIn profiles to at most 30 profiles, all belonging to people known to the author who gave 
informed consent for FYP testing (purpose explained, data stored securely and not published in the repository). 

For this FYP, any data in the evaluation was either synthetic, anonymised, or from the author themself. We had contacted a maximum of 30
individuals who gave informed consent for FYP testing, giving consent for the use of their CV, as well as Linkedin and GitHub profiles,
where available. Results are stored in Supabase, not published, and the API key is stored in local environment variables to ensure
security. While consent makes research more reasonable, it does not make scraping acceptible under LinkedIn's terms of service. Apify is not 
an appropriate way to scrape LinkedIn profiles in production, and would lead to legal issues. Alongside this, real deployment would require privacy 
impact assessments as well as processes in place for access, correction and deletion requests \cite{ico_employment_2023}. MERIT is build
with sensible defaults such as structured storage, Blind Mode, explainable scores, and a decision-support approach. However, meeting the 
law still depends on how the employer runs the process.
