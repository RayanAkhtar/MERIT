\chapter{Introduction}

This report presents the design, architecture, and evaluation of \textbf{M.E.R.I.T} (Multi-source Employment Ranking \& Insight Tool),
a customisable candidate screening system designed to modernise technical recruitment by moving beyond a single-source dependency.
Traditional technical recruitment is almost always dependent on the curriculum vitae (CV), which is a self-reported document, submitted by a 
candidate in a job application, in which they provide information about their education, work experience, skills, and other relevant information.
Reliance on a single source of information, especially one as unstructured and unreliable as the CV, is a major limitation of traditional
recruitment processes. MERIT~\cite{merit_repo} aims to address this obstacle through the integration of demonstrated evidence as well as structured data 
to provide a more accurate and fair assessment of a candidate's skill and experience.

Over the past few years, there has been a rapid increase in the number of technical roles globally, leading to intensified competition
among candidates and increasing the volume of applicants that hiring managers must screen. Organisations often attempt to streamline
early-stage screening by requiring concise CV formats. While the one-page resume is standard in the US and for entry-level roles,
UK standards often allow for two pages, particularly in more experienced, technical roles. Regardless of the length requirement imposed 
in the UK and US, research often shows that CV-based screening is still demanding, even if recruiters are only given a single page. 
Due to the cognitive demands of screening, recruiters will often lean on heuristics rather than an accurate assessment, and are 
thus prone to biases \cite{moore2023, tversky1974}.

The rapid nature of manual screening often introduces decision fatigue, which leads to the amplification of the cognitive biases already
present in screening. One example of this that we can turn to is the contrast effect, in which candidate evaluation can be influenced
by the relative quality of preceding candidates \cite{palmer2014}. In the domain of technical recruitment, skills are significantly
harder to assess, and as such, these effects are further exacerbated.

Another downside of the current state of recruitment lies in the nature of the CV, particularly due to its reliability. Candidates
may inflate their responsibilities or omit weaknesses in order to present themselves as more favourable to recruiters
\cite{henle2019_resume_fraud,dunning2004}. Candidates may also overestimate their abilities when self-reporting skills \cite{dunning2004}. 
In addition to overestimations, there is also a lack of accountability in the natural decay of their skills over time \cite{arthur1998}, 
further reducing the reliability of a manual CV-based screening process.


To address these challenges, many organisations adopt automated or semi-automated screening systems, which rank candidates based
on keywords, education, and work experience, or generate binary pass/fail outcomes. While these systems reduce recruiter workload
and accelerate the hiring process, they introduce severe limitations. They frequently ignore ``new talent signals'', such as project
portfolios, open-source contributions, or other verifiable demonstrations of ability that provide a superior assessment of technical
skills \cite{chamorro2016}. By overlooking these signals and relying on rigid scoring frameworks, automated systems systematically
underestimate qualified candidates and reinforce a narrow, one-size-fits-all approach. Another core issue is the lack of transparency 
in most systems, leaving recruiters with little or no insight into why a candidate may pass or fail. This 
black-box nature reduces trust, and also makes it hard for hiring managers to audit the fairness of automated hiring 
decisions \cite{raghavan2020}.


Automated screening platforms have been shown to inherit and propagate biases present in historical hiring data. Gender,
ethnicity, and institutional prestige can heavily influence a model's output, reproducing or even worsening the systemic discrimination present
in manual processes \cite{raghavan2020, bogen2018}. Most commercial tools also lack flexibility, applying identical scoring
frameworks to all candidates, limiting a recruiter's ability to prioritise role-specific competencies \cite{chamorro2016}.

Beyond algorithmic bias, automated screening is severely hindered by the technical fragility of the document parsing layer.
Most commercial screening systems rely on coordinate-based spatial PDF parsing libraries, such as PDFMiner \cite{pdfminer}, to
ingest unstructured CV text. These parsers frequently fail when processing modern two-column or multi-column layouts, horizontally
interleaving adjacent columns into single, corrupted strings and causing critical experience and skill details to be entirely
missed \cite{emnlp2025resumebench}. Consequently, highly qualified candidates are systematically penalised unless their CV formats 
are explicitly restricted to simple, linear designs. This layout-driven technical fragility introduces a profound, non-meritocratic 
selection bias, directly justifying the need to supplement traditional CVs with structured, verified external data sources.


The vulnerabilities to single-source evaluation, the presence of bias, and the threat of self-embellishment all point towards
the need for a multi-source, adaptable and explainable system that can assess technical skills while mitigating bias. High-validity 
selection methods have been shown to improve employee performance and productivity, leading to measurable economic and organisational 
benefits \cite{schmidt1998}. An accurate, rigorous early-stage screening process is therefore critical to reduce the likelihood of a bad hire.

MERIT addresses these issues through the combination of traditional CV data with verified behavioural and proficiency-based
evidence from multiple sources, such as GitHub and LinkedIn. By focusing on observable performance and providing transparent,
explainable justifications for its rankings, MERIT aims to reduce bias, improve reliability, and aid evidence-based
decision-making in technical recruitment.


\section{Contributions}
MERIT attempts to address the limitations of both traditional and modern automated screening systems, targeting the
rigidity of metrics, single-source dependency, as well as the lack of openness with regards to the scoring process.
Our contributions to this domain revolve around three architectural pillars, one for each stage of a typical screening pipeline (input
ingestion, scoring, and explainability). The pillars aim to overcome the limitations of automated hiring.

\subsubsection{Pillar 1: Extensibility \& Customisable Evaluation}
The first pillar aims to establish a dynamic, recruitment-aligned foundation by inferring screening metrics as well as target skills
directly from the job descriptions. Rather than applying a simple one-size-fits-all template, MERIT instead allows recruiters to 
rely on insights suggested by the model based on current recruitment data, however it is up to the recruiter to accept these metrics,
or to choose their own. This customisable metric extraction layer ensures flexibility within the technical hiring domain, while still
ensuring that MERIT is specific enough to capture the metrics that would be necessary for most technical professions, providing a 
highly flexible baseline for candidate ranking.

\subsubsection{Pillar 2: Multi-Source Evidence Fusion}
The second pillar of MERIT aims to address the majority of the aforementioned issues. Firstly, it attempts to overcome the 
unreliability of candidate data through the creation of a screening system that allows evidence from multiple data sources.
By integrating evidence-driven sources such as GitHub, we can evaluate candidates on demonstrated evidence, as opposed to claimed
evidence on their CV.

The next issue this pillar will attempt to address is the vulnerabilities of unstructured data parsing. Often, ATS systems
involve the parsing of CVs, however, to this day, there are significant issues with CV parsing \cite{emnlp2025resumebench}. Through the 
integration of a structured data source such as LinkedIn, we can provide supplementary data to aid a candidate's application in the 
event of malformed CV parsing.

By taking both of these factors into consideration, as well as the application of temporal skill decay and multi-source conflict
resolution, MERIT mitigates the impact of resume exaggerations as well as missing information, to help capture a more holistic
view of a candidate and their technical proficiency.

\subsubsection{Pillar 3: The Explainability Layer}
The final pillar revolves around trust, auditability and transparency, to combat the limitations of black-box screening systems.
MERIT abandons unexplained, arbitrary numerical grading that lacks significance in order to ensure that recruiters can truly
understand why a candidate was given a certain rank by MERIT. One of the main contributions of this pillar is the audit trail
generation, providing a transparent and traceable explanation behind candidate scoring. This contribution is supplemented with
the addition of game-theoretic scoring through Shapley values in order to provide a more fair and accurate assessment of a candidate's abilities.
We pair these contributions with interactive dashboard, clear visualisation, and also candidate anonymisation to reduce the risk
of human bias in the screening process.

By combining these three pillars, MERIT is able to provide a more fair and accurate assessment of a candidate's abilities
by shifting away from self-reported skill inflation and CV formatting biases. By introducing candidate anonymisation and
standardised formatting of CV data, we aim to further reduce this risk of bias. These pillars will also
assist in providing complete transparency on automated screening through interactive, source-traceable recruiter reports.


\section{Report Structure}
This report will take the following structure:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Chapter 2: Background} outlines the standard procedures, cognitive demands, and systemic biases 
  inherent in manual recruitment.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 3: Technical Foundations \& Related Work} examines both traditional and AI-assisted automated 
  screening systems, the risks of automated bias propagation, and existing work in the field of technical recruitment that applies to MERIT.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 4: Implementation} provides an in-depth look at the architecture behind MERIT. We cover the full process,
  from the ingestion pipeline, to the scoring algorithms, as well as the explainability layer.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 5: Evaluation} presents the quantitative and qualitative findings validating the three core 
  architectural pillars of the system to evaluate the trade-offs present in following MERIT's architecture.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 6: Ethics} discusses key societal, legal, and operational considerations surrounding algorithmic hiring,
  as well as the ethical implications of using MERIT in the hiring process.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 7: Critical Reflection \& Future Work} provides a pillar-aligned retrospective on evaluation
  outcomes, design trade-offs, and methodological scope, together with a prioritised research and deployment roadmap.
  \item \textbf{Chapter 8: Conclusion} delivers a final verdict on whether MERIT achieved its three pillars
  (extensible, multi-source, explainable) as an auditable screening prototype.
\end{itemize}
