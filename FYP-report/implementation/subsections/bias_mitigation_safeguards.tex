\section{Bias Mitigation \& Safeguards}
\label{sec:bias-mitigation-safeguards}

To ensure accurate candidate evaluation while ensuring the integrity of the screening process,
MERIT incorporates a range of safeguards. These mechanisms are designed to mitigate
both algorithmic gaming (e.g. keyword stuffing) and also human unconscious bias
(e.g. institutional or educational prestige).

\subsection{Algorithmic Safeguards: The Integrity Engine}
MERIT's backend includes an Integrity Engine, the purpose of which is to audit candidate data for both
consistency as well as authenticity, flagging any inconsistencies or anomalies that impact the final
score.

\subsubsection{Anti-Keyword Stuffing Heuristics}
Keyword stuffing is a common threat in automated CV screenings, where candidates often attempt to inflate their
scores by repeating job related buzzwords. To counter this on the CV level, MERIT implements an algorithm to
penalise repeated keywords with a linear point deduction for each instance beyond a certain threshold.
Refer to Listing \ref{lst:stuffing_logic} for the implementation details.
\begin{moderncode}[title={Keyword Stuffing Penalty Logic}]
    \label{lst:stuffing_logic}
    \begin{lstlisting}[language=json]
"ANTI_STUFFING": {
    "OCCURRENCE_LIMIT": 5,          // Repetitions allowed per CV
    "PENALTY_PER_OCCURRENCE": 0.05, // 5% reduction per excess
    "MAX_TOTAL_PENALTY": 0.60       // Penalty cap
}
\end{lstlisting}
\end{moderncode}


If a keyword appears more than five times in a CV, the system flags the specific metric (such as Python) and
applies a linear penalty. This encourages candidates to provide meaningful context rather than lexical repetition.
In practice the threshold would be higher, and the penalty would be lower to avoid excessive penalisation, however
this demonstrates the logical implementation.


\subsubsection{Identity Consistency Audit (The Squatter Defense)}
When candidates apply for roles, they often have multiple copies of their own CV, tailored to specific roles. While
it is ideal to increase the reach of a candidate, candidates often apply for roles that they are not qualified for,
embellishing claims to appear as a good fit. It is reasonable to assume that candidates may also consider "identity squatting"
using someone else's portfolio, and passing it off as their own.

To prevent "identity squatting" or the use of fraudulent social profiles, the system performs a fuzzy name-match between the
CV and the provided GitHub/LinkedIn profiles. We perform a fuzzy match to account for middle names, initials, and other minor variations.
Any discrepancies are surfaced in the UI, allowing the recruiter to exercise human judgment for edge cases (e.g. legal name changes).

In terms of implementation, we rely on the \textit{difflib} library, more specifically its \texttt{SequenceMatcher} class to calculate a
similarity ratio between the candidate's name on their CV, and their name on the other data sources. If this ratio falls below 0.7, a
flat \textbf{20\% Integrity Tax} is applied to the final score. This mechanism attempts to combat the potential for account squatting due to the
multi-source nature of MERIT.

\subsubsection{Source Trust Anchoring: Claimed vs. Demonstrated Evidence}
To counter "Self-Report Bias", we leverage our Bayesian Evidence Fusion mechanism. For example, when it comes to extensible metrics,
we place a higher degree of trust in GitHub (0.9 weight) compared to CV (0.5 weight) or LinkedIn (0.2 weight). This ensures that
candidates who possess demonstrated evidence are favoured in scoring, as opposed to candidates who may claim skills on their CV without
the evidence to back it up. This weight selection also possesses a secondary benefit of combating keyword stuffing.

\subsection{Blind Screening Mode \& Anonymisation}
While the backend focuses on technical integrity, the frontend addresses human-centric risks through a toggleable \textbf{Blind Mode} (Figure \ref{fig:blind_mode_ui}).

\subsubsection{PII Redaction \& Identity Masking}
Personally Identifiable Information (PII) refers to information that can be used to identify an individual, with common examples including names and
email addresses. As explored in the background section, such markers can lead to unconscious biases regarding a candidate's race, gender, or religion.
When active, MERIT redacts all PII alongside proxy variables for protected characteristics, such as profile pictures and institutional names. 
The per-candidate \textbf{match score and Glass-Box skill breakdown remain visible}: Blind Mode does not suppress algorithmic outputs, so recruiters always see MERIT's quantitative guidance during popup review (Study~15, Section~\ref{subsec:bias-mitigation-efficacy}).

This is performed through a custom React component \texttt{renderRedactedText} which wraps sensitive strings. More specifically, our system anonymises:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Full Names \& Emails:} Replaced with generic identifiers (e.g., "Candidate \#1").
    \item \textbf{Institutional Data:} School and university names are redacted to prevent "prestige bias" from
          influencing the recruiter's perception. Note that the tier ranking (e.g. "Tier 1") is preserved
    \item \textbf{Avatars:} GitHub and LinkedIn profile photos are replaced with generic placeholders.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Visual De-biasing of Original Documents}
In the overlay for a candidate's report, it is possible for the recruiter to access and view the original CV. This is often necessary in the event of
data not being extracted properly. As shown in the background section, recruiters may also hold biases towards specific styles and structures of CVs.
To mitigate this bias, under blind mode, the original CV is not displayed. Instead, the recruiter is presented with the extracted data in a
neutral format that is consistent across all candidates. This forces the recruiter to rely on the objective, skill-based evidence, rather than visual
layouts or potentially biased markers within the text document (such as keywords being bolded).
