\section{Bias Mitigation \& Algorithmic Safeguards}
\label{sec:bias-mitigation-safeguards}

Similar to current screening systems, if MERIT were to be deployed, candidates would likely attempt to game 
the system. In a similar manner, recruiters may also fall on automation bias, or other forms of unconscious bias,
to justify a potential decision that they had already intended on making. The following mechanisms are designed
in order to account for the potential expected behaviour of these candidates and recruiters.


\subsection{The Integrity Engine}
MERIT's backend includes an Integrity Engine, the purpose of which is to audit candidate data for both
consistency as well as authenticity, flagging any inconsistencies or anomalies that impact the final
score.

\subsubsection{Keyword Stuffing}
Keyword stuffing is a common threat in automated CV screenings, where candidates often attempt to inflate their
scores by repeating job related buzzwords. MERIT penalises repeated keywords once occurrences exceed a configurable
threshold (currently five per keyword in a CV), applying a 5\% score reduction per additional repetition up to a
60\% cap on the total stuffing penalty so as not to severely punish candidates who repeat terms within reason. Note that penalties only apply to the 
CV signal, so in the current state of MERIT, it would be possible to recover from keyword stuffing penalisations if a 
candidate is able to demonstrate strong evidence of their claims in other sources.


\subsubsection{Identity Squatting}
When candidates apply for roles, they often have multiple copies of their own CV, tailored to specific roles. While
it is ideal to increase the reach of a candidate, candidates often apply for roles that they are not qualified for,
embellishing claims to appear as a good fit. It is reasonable to assume that candidates may also consider ``identity squatting''
using someone else's portfolio, and passing it off as their own.

To prevent ``identity squatting'' or the use of fraudulent social profiles, the system performs a fuzzy name-match between the
CV and the provided GitHub/LinkedIn profiles. To account for minor variations in details such as middle names, initials, titles, etc., 
we perform a fuzzy match. After performing this, we surface the result in the UI, allowing the recruiter to have the final say in
whether the candidate is a squatter or not.

In terms of implementation, we rely on the \textit{difflib} library, more specifically its \texttt{SequenceMatcher} class to calculate a
similarity ratio between the candidate's name on their CV, and their name on the other data sources. If this ratio falls below 0.7, a
flat \textbf{20\% Integrity Tax} is applied to the final score. Identity squatting will likely be a common threat with MERIT with the 
introduction of more data sources, so it is better to have a safeguard in place to account for malicious actors.

\subsubsection{Claimed vs. Demonstrated Evidence}
One of the main reasons we considered MERIT to have a pillar of multi-source fusion was to account for the potential of ``Self-Report Bias''.
To combat this issue, we rely on our Bayesian Evidence Fusion mechanism to reward candidates more for demonstrated evidence.
For example, when it comes to extensible metrics, we place a higher degree of trust in GitHub (0.9 weight) compared to CV 
(0.5 weight) or LinkedIn (0.2 weight). This ensures that candidates who possess demonstrated evidence are favoured in 
scoring, as opposed to candidates who may claim skills on their CV without the evidence to back it up. This weight selection
also possesses a secondary benefit of combating keyword stuffing.

\subsection{Blind Mode \& Anonymisation}
The backend's job is to focus on technical integrity, ensuring that data is extracted correctly and consistently.
The goal of the frontend is to focus on human-centric risks, specifically human bias. One of the main ways we try to 
address this is through the implementation of \textbf{Blind Mode} (Figure \ref{fig:blind_mode_ui}).

\subsubsection{PII Redaction}
Personally Identifiable Information (PII) refers to information that can be used to identify an individual, with common examples including names and
email addresses. As explored in the background section, such markers can lead to unconscious biases regarding a candidate's race, gender, or religion.
When active, MERIT redacts all PII alongside proxy variables for protected characteristics, such as profile pictures and institutional names. 
Note that in blind mode, we still show both the candidate rank and match score. While this is an intentional design choice, we must note that
this can introduce automation bias, a situation where the recruiter may anchor on the engine scores and glass-box breakdowns rather than 
re-evaluating their own judgments from redacted text alone.

Our way of implementing blind mode is to create a custom React component \textit{renderRedactedText} which 
wraps sensitive strings. More specifically, our system anonymises:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Full Names \& Emails:} Replaced with generic identifiers (e.g.\ ``Candidate \#1'').
    \item \textbf{Institutional Data:} School and university names are redacted to prevent ``prestige bias'' from
          influencing the recruiter's perception. Note that the tier ranking (e.g.\ ``Tier 1'') is preserved.
    \item \textbf{Avatars:} GitHub and LinkedIn profile photos are replaced with generic placeholders.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Visual De-biasing}
In the overlay for a candidate's report, it is possible for the recruiter to access and view the original CV. This is often necessary in the event of
data not being extracted properly. As shown in the background section, recruiters may also hold biases towards specific styles and structures of CVs.
To mitigate this bias, under blind mode, the original CV is not displayed. Instead, the recruiter is presented with the extracted data in a
neutral format that is consistent across all candidates. This forces the recruiter to rely on the objective, skill-based evidence, rather than visual
layouts or potentially biased markers within the text document (such as keywords being bolded).
