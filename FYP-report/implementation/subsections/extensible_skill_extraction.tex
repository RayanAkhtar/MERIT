\section{Stage 1: Data Extraction and Job Configuration}
The first stage of the MERIT pipeline focuses on \textit{Data Extraction} and \textit{Job Configuration}.
Here, we translate unstructured job requirements into a structured, weighted mathematically-backed profile.
This stage is critical for establishing the system's "Integrity Signal". Rather than requiring
recruiters to manually define every skill and priority, MERIT leverages automated extraction
and market-aware weighting to pre-fill the job's configuration.
This process, illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:skill_extraction_pipeline}, ensures that the system remains
extensible to any technical domain while significantly reducing the manual overhead of
job setup.

\subsection{Entity Extraction \& Preprocessing}
Before we perform semantic analysis, we first need to extract the relevant technical
skills from the job descriptions and CVs. For MERIT, we have implemented a heuristic
entity extraction method that utilises a curated dictionary of over 500 technical
languages and frameworks (stored in \texttt{skills\_data.json}). This system identifies
raw mentions of these terms within the text. This helps us isolate technical signals from general
prose, to ensure that the semantic matcher only focuses on relevant skills. Another purpose of this
preprocessing step is to resolve common variations of the same skill. For example, the term
"JS" is often used interchangeably with "JavaScript" in job descriptions. By identifying and
normalising these variations, we prevent the inclusion of unnecessary noise during the vector similarity calculation.

\subsection{Semantic Skill Matching}
After we have performed a relatively rigid entity extraction, we move onto the
semantic matching stage. Here, we address the limitations of traditional Applicant
Tracking Systems (ATS) that rely on exact string matching. We are able to achieve this through
the use of Latent Space Mathematics. Rather than following traditional ATS systems that rely on
exact string matching, we instead rely on vector-based similarity analysis.

We use the \textit{SentenceTransformers} library, and the \textit{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} model for this process.
This model was selected for its balance between efficiency and accuracy for technical terminology.
We encode terms into a 384-dimensional dense vector space, capturing the underlying semantic relationships
between technologies. This is achieved through the application of the \textbf{cosine similarity}
formula as demonstrated below.

\begin{equation}
    \text{similarity} = \cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B}}{\|\mathbf{A}\| \|\mathbf{B}\|}
\end{equation}

For MERIT, we set the threshold to $0.50$, this was determined through iterative testing and
comparison with the results of the heuristic entity extraction method. This threshold is
significant enough to determine that "SQL" and "PostgreSQL" are similar skills, while not being
too strict as to reject semantically similar terms such as "React" and "React.js"


\subsection{Dynamic Weighting via TF-IDF}
Another key innovation towards the extensibility of MERIT lies in the inference of skill importance
when setting weights during the configuration stage. This is achieved through the application of a
modified Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) approach. This aligns with the
"Information-theoretic Analysis of Skill Importance" \cite{zhang2019skill}, which suggests that
skills with high Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) are significantly more "informative" for
classifying a job role than common terms.

We calculate the TF-IDF scores based on both the job description, as well as the market corpus.
The \textbf{Term Frequency (TF)}, or "Job Intensity," reflects the employer's subjective
emphasis \cite{kou2020mcdm} and is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    TF(s, J) = \frac{f_{s, J}}{\sum_{k \in J} w_k}
\end{equation}

Where $f_{s, J}$ is the number of times skill $s$ is mentioned in the job description
$J$, and $\sum w_k$ is the total word count of the document. Simultaneously, the \textbf{Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)},
or "Market Scarcity," is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
    IDF(s, D) = \log\left(\frac{N}{1 + df(s)}\right) + 1
\end{equation}

Where $N$ is the total number of job descriptions in our market corpus $D$, and $df(s)$ is the
number of documents containing skill $s$ \cite{bastian2014linkedin}. The final significance score
$W_s$ for each skill is the product of these two components:

\begin{equation}
    W_s = TF(s, J) \times IDF(s, D)
\end{equation}

The resulting significance score is linearly mapped to a $1$ to $5$ priority scale.
This ensures that if a recruiter asks for a common skill like "Python" and a more niche skill
like "Jupyter Notebook" the system prioritises the rarer skill unless the JD explicitly
emphasises "Python" through high repetition density.
