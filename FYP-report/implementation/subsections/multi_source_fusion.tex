\section{Stage 2: Multi-Source Evidence Fusion \& Scoring}
Once candidate data and a job description have been extracted and combined in a configuration, we can then proceed to
the execution stage. In this stage, we compute signals for each metric. This can be done in two different ways
depending on whether the metric is baseline or extensible. Both metrics may take into account multiple data sources,
computing up to one signal per data source per metric. If this is the case, we perform bayesian fusion to compute a single
score. Once each metric has been reduced to a single value, we then undergo a weighted average of all the metrics
to compute the final (match) score. Figure \ref{fig:schema_overview} shows how we can use a candidate's
profile alongside a job description to produce a final score.

\subsection{Metric Metrics: Baseline vs. Extensible}
When executing a job configuration, the syste, categorises evaluation criteria against two different kinds of metrics.
\textbf{Baseline Metrics} are pre-defined metrics that are common to all configurations, and typically follow the industry-standard
heuristics. For example, a candidate's education level is a universal factor in technical recruitment that can be assessed
independently of the specific job requirements. \textbf{Extensible Metrics}, on the other hand, are job-specific metrics, in other words,
they are directly inferred from the Job Description. An example of an extensible metric for a typical "Machine Learning Engineer"
may be "Python", which would ideally measure the candidate's skill level, or usage of the programming language. By combining these
two classes of metrics, we are able to produce a holistic, yet highly targeted evaluation of a candidate.

For both classes of metrics, the system may take into account multiple data sources. Some of these sources may self-reported
(such as CV and LinkedIn) while other sources may be evidence-driven (such as GitHub). After reducing these signals to a single score,
we perform a weighted average of all the metrics to compute the final (match) score.


\subsection{Adversarial Mitigation \& Anti-Stuffing Heuristics}
A common flaw in legacy ATS platforms lies in their vulnerability to adversarial attacks. One such attack is keyword-stuffing,
a tactic where candidates artificially inflate their scores for individual metrics by repeatedly embedding target keywords
within their CV, either explicity as black text, or implicitly, using either white-text, or other similar methods. To combat
this specifical adversarial attack, we follow the industry-standard of applying penalties for repeated stuffing of keywords
within a CV.

For other data sources beyond the CV, such as LinkedIn and GitHub, we cannot follow these standards as current ATS platforms
do not yet support these data sources. For this reason, we have developed out own methods of adversarial mitigation to
anticipate the unique vulnerabilities of these data sources.

\subsubsection{Account Squatting Defense}
Account Squatting, is the name we have given to the tactic of a candidate attempting to impersonate another user, by passing off
their profile as their own. To provide a realistic example, if a user applies for a job as a "Machine Learning Engineer" but their
GitHub profile currently shows a proficiency in the area of "Full-Stack Development", a candidate may be likely to game the system
by providing a different GitHub URL that highlights another user's experience, thereby "Squatting" another user's profile.

To combat this, we perform an identity consistency audit. This method attempts to cross-reference
provided data sources, such as LinkedIn and GitHub by performing a fuzzy match on the full name for the associated profile.
If the similarity ratio between a given data source's name and the name of a candidate is below a certain threshold
(in our case $0.70$), we will apply a flat $20\%$ penalty to the overall profile score. This threshold is weak enough to account
for differences in middle names, or seniority titles (e.g. "Dr."), but strict enough to catch adversarial actors attempting to game
the system by providing different data sources based on the role or job title. It is important to note that the penalty is noted
on the candidate's profile so that recruiters are aware of potential adversarial activity and can investigate whether or not this
is the case.

\subsubsection{Temporal Skill Decay Modelling}
Skill Decay, first coined by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885, refers to the study of the rate at which a skill or
knowledge is lost over time due to a lack of practice or usage. When it comes to the context of technical recruitment, it is
clear that recency of experience matters. A candidate who used a certain programming language 5 years ago does not carry
the same weight as a candidate who used that same programming language a few months ago. A current limitation of traditional
ATS platforms is their failure to account for this.

To address this concern, MERIT implements an Exponential Decay Model for technical skills, inspired by classical models of memory
retention \cite{ebbinghaus1885memory}. For every skill signal extracted, its base value is penalised based on the time elapsed
since it was last utilised. The decayed signal strength, $\mathcal{S}_{\text{decay}}$, is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{S}_{\text{decay}} = \mathcal{S}_{\text{base}} \cdot e^{-\lambda \Delta t}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\mathcal{S}_{\text{base}}$ is the initial magnitude of the extracted skill signal.
  \item $\Delta t$ is the time elapsed (in years) since the end date of the relevant
        experience or repository commit.
  \item $\lambda$ is the decay constant, determining the rate of skill degradation.
\end{itemize}

The system is configured with a default exponential decay constant of $\lambda = 0.25$,
translating to an approximate half-life of $2.8$ years for technical skills. This temporal
adjustment ensures that recent, relevant experience naturally outranks historical, potentially
obsolete capabilities.

\subsection{Source-Aware Signal Aggregation}
When it comes to evaluating a candidate, there are two forms of evidence that we can use to evaluate them.
The first form of evidence is \textbf{Claimed Evidence}, where the candidate explicitly declares to possess
a certain skill or experience. This form of evidence is commonly found in CVs and LinkedIn profiles, and is often
the only form of evidence available to traditional ATS platforms. A major limitation to this form of evidence lies in
the potential for self-embellishment, as candidates may be incentivised to overstate their abilities to
secure a position.

To account for this, we turn to the second form of evidence, \textbf{Demonstrated Evidence}. This form of evidence is
derived from auditable, historical actions, specifically GitHub repositories. To provide an example, a candidate may
claim on their CV that they possess expertise in Python. However, this claim can then be challenged by the
presence of their GitHub repositories, specifically looking at the candidate's general language distribution throughout their
GitHub profile. Demonstrated evidence is significantly harder to game, as it requires a candidate to
possess actual experience in a skill to demonstrate it.

This formal separation of evidence is key to MERIT's "Evidence-based evaluation" ethos, and will play a key role in Bayesian Fusion.

\subsection{Bayesian Evidence Fusion}
The most significant innovation in MERIT's Multi-Source Fusion module lies in the adoption of \textbf{Bayesian Evidence Fusion}.
Initial iterations of MERIT attempted to combine skill signals using static weighted averages. However, this approach often dilutes
the importance of demonstrated evidence (GitHub) with claimed evidence (CV and LinkedIn). This approach also failed to account for the
confidence of a measurement. For example, a single line in a CV stating "Python" does not carry the same weight as a GitHub profile
that demonstrates extensive Python experience.

Another key issue is the lack of flexibility it provides, as we would now need separate definitions to account for candidates who may not
have all three data sources. Several considered approaches failed to provide a fair solution whilst adhering to the static weighted average
approach. Alongside this, there was also the issue of extensibility, as we need to provide $N!$ distinct definitions for $N$ data sources if
we wish to avoid penalising candidates for missing data sources.

The presence of these dilemmas led to the adoption of \textbf{Bayesian Evidence Fusion}. The current iteration of MERIT now treats each
data source as independent evidence updating a Beta Distribution. We parameterise this with the use of a sparse prior
($\alpha_{prior} = 0.1, \beta_{prior} = 0.1$) to ensure high sensitivity to initial evidence signals while maintaining a baseline for uncertainty.

The contribution of each source $s$ to the final score of that metric is given by its raw signal $\sigma_s \in [0, 1]$ and its reliability weight $w_s$.
Evidence-based data sources are often assigned a higher weight due to MERIT's emphasis on evidence-based screening. To encourage candidates to
use evidence-based sources while avoiding harsh penalisation, we enforce a "Signal Cap" for sources that provide claimed evidence.
We then map these signals to the evidence parameters as follows:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_s = w_s \sigma_s + \epsilon_s, \quad \beta_s = w_s (1 - \sigma_s) + \epsilon_s
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_s = 0.01(1 - w_s)$ represents a symmetric uncertainty term. The reason for the inclusion of $\epsilon_s$ is to emulate inject noise for
low-confidence sources to produce wider, more uncertain distributions. This stops the distribution from becoming narrow unless this candidate has the
evidence to support their claims (since high-confidence sources provice more concerntrated signals.)

We select the Beta distribution since it is the conjugate prior for Bernoulli-distributed evidence. This means that the resulting posterior
update still remains a Beta distribution. This allos for additive evidence integration, which is, not only computationally more efficient,
but also extensible for an arbitrary set of data sources $S$. This aligns with the "Extensibility" goal of MERIT. The updates to the Beta
distribution are given by:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{total} = \alpha_{prior} + \sum_{s \in S} \alpha_s, \quad \beta_{total} = \beta_{prior} + \sum_{s \in S} \beta_s
\end{equation}

The final posterior distribution is:
\begin{equation}
  P(\theta \mid \text{Evidence}) \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_{total}, \beta_{total})
\end{equation}

This probabilistic approach provides a robust framework for modeling uncertainty and resolving conflicts between
different evidence sources. For a verified signal, if a candidate has claimed evidence on a CV (low weight) and also
possesses the demonstrated evidence to back it up, such as on GitHub (high weight), then the resulting distribution will
skew towards a high probability of the candidate possessing the skill, with low variance. On the other hand, if a skill is listed
heavily on their CV, but absent from their GitHub, the $\alpha$ parameter will only increase slightly. The baseline $\beta$ parameter
will then prevent the score from artificially inflating, mathematically mitigating "keyword stuffing" even further.

Once we have computed the posterior distribution for a metric, we can then split it into it's expected value and it's variance. Effectively
this measures what the candidate's proficiency in a certain skill is, and the confidence that we have in that claim. For a random variable
$\theta \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, we define it's expected value $E[\theta]$ and variance $\text{Var}(\theta)$ as:
\begin{equation}
  E[\theta] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}, \quad \text{Var}(\theta) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha + \beta)^2(\alpha + \beta + 1)}
\end{equation}

To conclude, the expected value of this distribution is the candidate's final fused score for the metric in question.
The ranking system, as a result, aims to prioritise candidates with verified evidence across multiple sources over sources
which only provide claimed evidence. However, this is not strict enough to entirely rule out candidates with claimed evidence
who may not have evidence to back it up.





\subsection{Result Payload Architecture: The Glass-Box Proof}
The majority of modern ATS platforms receive criticism for being black-box in nature. It is often unclear to recruiters exactly
how a candidate was ranked, nor can it delve into the specific metrics that contributed to a candidate's final score. MERIT aims to
challenge this paradigm by returning a comprehensive JSON payload (\texttt{results\_payload}) that serves
as a mathematical audit trail. This captures the lineage of a recruitment decision, from the raw data being extracted,
to bayesian fusion. This includes any penalties incurred during the scoring process.
We also store a lightweight \texttt{summary\_data} object\footnote{The \texttt{summary\_data} field is a lightweight UI-optimisation
  feature designed to populate recruiter dashboards without the network latency of fetching the complete, multi-megabyte
  \texttt{results\_payload}.}, which contains high-level
metadata (such as the top candidate and score) for rapid batch retrieval without
re-processing the full evaluation payload.

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:schema_overview}, this payload is the terminal product
of the execution stage. Its hierarchical structure ensures that every high-level score
is supported by granular evidence chunks. We categorise this schema into three functional layers.

The first layer is the aggregation layer, which contains the overall score and the
calculation summary, providing a high-level overview and the global weighted
formula used. The second layer is the metric layer. This contains the specific evaluation criteria
(e.g., \textit{Experience}, \textit{req\_Python}). Each entry contains its independent
Bayesian parameters ($\alpha, \beta$) and its fused score. The third layer is the evidence layer,
which is the most granular level, found within\texttt{source\_details}. This contains the specific
string derivations, source confidence coefficients, and temporal decay penalties applied to individual signals.

architecture. By exposing this data through the system's "Insight" panel in the
frontend, MERIT transforms a binary hiring decision into a defensible, auditable
