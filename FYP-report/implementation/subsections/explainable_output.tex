\section{Stage 3: Explainable Interpretation \& Visualisation}
The last stage of MERIT takes place once we have finished computing metrics for all of the candidates in the job configuration.
In this stage, we translate the complex internal mathematical states into transparent human-readable audit trails, as well as
simple visualisations to aid readability and understanding. MERIT aim of being a glass-box recruitment depends upon this stage,
as we need to ensure that recruiters are not just given a final score, but also the quantitative reasoning behind that score to either
trust, or even challenge candidate scores. We aid this by also providing probabilistic confidence scoring, applying Cooperative Game
Theory for data source contributions both to the candidate's final score, as well as to their individual metrics.

\subsection{Probabilistic Scoring \& Confidence Intervals}
As mentioned in the previous chapter, Bayesian Evidence Fusion not only provides an expected value, but also a variance, in other words,
the certainty of that candidate's score. Two candidates may have a similar expected score for a metrics, but could have vastly different
levels of confidence due to the sources of information for that metric. For example, one candiate may have verified signals across multiple
sources, while another candidate may only have a single, unverified claim from a low-trust source. It is important that a recruiter is able
to know of this, and in a simple manner.

We defined how variance is computed in section 4.3.4. We build upon this by now computing the confidence interval for that candidate's score.
Firstly, we calculate the standard deviation as $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(\theta)}$ to quantify this uncertainty. In our implementation of
MERIT, we avoid displaying confidence intervals to avoid cognitive overload for the recruiter. Instead, we our confidence in a candidates
score by outputting a label: "High Confidence", "Medium Confidence", or "Low Confidence".

We define "High Confidence" as a standard deviation $\sigma < 0.20$, "Medium Confidence" as $0.20 \le \sigma < 0.35$, and
"Low Confidence" as $\sigma \ge 0.35$.

By outputting intuitive labels, rather than raw intervals, recruiters can quickly scan whether a candidate's score is
backed by a variety of strong evidence.


\subsection{Shapley Value Integration}
While Bayesian Evidence Fusion and confidence scoring addresses the problem of understanding a candidate's proficiencies, we must address
the biggest disadvantage of this approach. Bayesian Evidence Fusion, at its core, is an aggregation mechanic that fuses multiple signals
together. We lose key information about the impact of each data source on that metric's final score. We cannot tell if a candidate got a high
score due to one very strong signal, or due to many weak signals. This is a problem that shatters the "Glass-box" goal of merit, so its vital
that we address this.

In a true "Glass-Box" system, recruiters must be able to understand the reasoning behind the scores, whether this was driven by demonstrated
evidence or claimed evidence. To solve this, turn towards cooperative game theory, namely Shapley values. When data sources contain
overlapping or redundant signals, naive percentage breakdowns fail to accurately share credit amongst data sources. Shapley values
overcome this through a mathematically rigorous method of fairly distributing the total "payoff" (the candidates score) amongst the
cooperating "players" (the data sources).

In MERIT's current implementation, we currently only support three data sources: CV, GitHub, and LinkedIn
($N = \{\text{CV}, \text{GitHub}, \text{LinkedIn}\}$, $n = 3$). The value $v(S)$ of a coalition of sources is the score that we achieve
when the candidate is evaluated using only the data from those sources, with non-coalition sources strictly masked. In other words, If we want
to compute the value of the coalition of sources {CV, GitHub}, we first take a candidate and provide the system with
their CV and GitHub. We then run the MERIT evaluation pipeline, but with the LinkedIn source strictly masked. This
means that any claims or signals coming from the LinkedIn profile will not be considered. The output of this
evaluation is the value of the coalition {CV, GitHub} for that candidate.

For the set of sources $N$, the number of possible coalitions is $2^n$. In our case, we have $2^3 = 8$ possible coalitions.
To arrive at the shapley value $\phi_i$ for a single source $i$, we must sum the marginal contributions
of that source across all coalitions that it is a part of, weighted by the size of the coalition. This can be
computed by the following equation:

\begin{equation}
    \phi_i = \sum_{S \subseteq N \setminus \{i\}} \frac{|S|! (n - |S| - 1)!}{n!} [v(S \cup \{i\}) - v(S)]
\end{equation}

By calculating $\phi_i$ for both individual metrics, as well as the final score, MERIT is able to attribute every score
to a data source, and inform the recruiter whether a candidate's score was driven by demonstrated evidence, or claimed evidence.

\subsection{Audit Trail Generation \& Algorithmic Contestability}
ATS platforms are often criticised for their lack of transparency and auditability. When a candidate is given a low score,
or rejected from an ATS, both candidates, as well as the recruiters have no insight as to why that was the case. Due to this,
neither party can learn from this interaction, or challenge the claims that the system makes.

It is for this reason that MERIT implements the principle of \textit{contestability}. Recruiters are not presented with
a "black-box" score, instead, they are given the specific evidence signals used to derive that score through a comprehensive
audit trail. This way, we can not only provide rankings (like all other ATS systems), but explain the quantitative
reasoning behind them. This end-to-end transparency allows a human-in-the-loop to identify and override potential
algorithmic edge cases, ensuring that the final decision remains human-centric and auditable.

During stage 2 of MERIT (the execution phase), our backend constructs a \texttt{calculation\_summary} along with detailed
metric breakdowns to populate the \texttt{results\_payload}. For each evaluated skill or heuristic, we provide a full
breakdown of the score, from the extracted signals, to the Bayesian Evidence Fusion process. The system explicitly documents
this logic. For a complete, step-by-step visual breakdown of the Verification Audit for a specific metric
(including temporal decay and Bayesian fusion), please refer to Appendix \ref{appendix:math_trail}. This explicitly demonstrates
how the level of confidence is derived, along with the Shapley Values for each data source.

This "Glass-Box" approach attempts to bridge the gap between complex probabilistic mathematics and practical recruitment
workflows, transforming a binary hiring decision into a defensible and interrogate-able process.


\subsection{Dynamic Cohort Normalisation}
Another common flaw with algorithmic scoring is the absense of relative context. A candidate with a year of experience may seem
weak on paper, but would be considered exceptional if the majority of the applicant pool had little to no experience. However, due
to this, the candidate would likely be undervalued if the system does not correctly highlight the difference in experience between
the candidate and the rest of the pool. To address this, MERIT also implements a dynamic cohort normalisation mechanic for the majority of
baseline metrics.

In order to achieve dynamic cohort normalisation, MERIT undergoes a two-pass approach. During the first pass of this stage, the backend
calculates the raw, absolute metrics for every candidate in the batch (such as Years of Experience, or Total GitHub repositories). Then
we aggregate these results to identify cohort maximums. In the second pass, we rerun the scoring engine, normalising candidate metrics against
the batch maximums. This approach, while not runtime efficient for extremely large datasets, ensures that MERIT's ranking remain adaptive
to the competitive landscape, rather than an arbitrary baseline.

\subsection{Interactive Visualisation}
Understandably, presenting the raw JSOB \textit{results\_payload} is unreasonable due to it being inherently unscalable and poor UX.
We will briedly go over the main features of our Next.js frontend to create an intuitive, visual "Glass-Box" dashboard.

The first feature is probabilistic confidence labels, derived from the standard deviation $\sigma$ of the Beta distribution mentioned
in the previous chapter. We map $\sigma$ to a traffic-light badge system (Green for High, Amber for Medium, Red for Low),
allowing recruiters to instantly scan the reliability of a candidate's metric at a glance. The significance of these labels is highlighted
more so when it comes to extensible metrics, where there is more uncertainty due to the ambiguity of how such metrics may be defined,
and calculated.

Another key innovation towards the UX of merit is the "Insights" panel. When viewing the ranking tables of a candidate, recruiters can open
a "report" for them, which will provide them with an overlay of, not only the candidate's score, but also how it was calculated. The metric's
calculation is also provided as a human-readable string, which can be used to understand the logic behind the score. To further aid transparency,
any penalisations such as (such as Squatting) are also flagged to the user to highlight the negative impact of such actions on the candidate's score.
Alongside this, it is also possible to see structured views of the candidate's provided data source (e.g. their CV, or a simplified
representation of their GitHub profile). This allows recruiters to cross-reference the system's claims with the candidate's actual provided data,
ensuring full transparency.
