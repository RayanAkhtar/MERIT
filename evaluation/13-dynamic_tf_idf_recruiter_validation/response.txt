What we were trying to answer
-----------------------------
Studies 04–05 already showed the parser can miss skills on messy PDFs. Study 13 is
different: assume the skill list is correct, and ask whether MERIT's TF-IDF engine
gives sensible 1–5 priorities compared to what recruiters would actually set.

So this is about dynamic_weighting + the 100-JD market corpus, not ingestion. We
kept it offline (no Supabase) and small on purpose — three hold-out roles, four
people rating them. Kinda a weakness since we are testing with a static corpus
this evaluation will likely have different results as more newer, trendier technologise 
come out. The importance of some languages to recruiters (such as python) could fall 
in the future as others come to replace it. So it may not be as repeatable 10 years from now, for example.


Setup (short version)
---------------------
- Corpus: 100 synthetic tech JDs for IDF only (recruiters never saw these).
- Hold-outs: Junior Frontend (React), Senior ML, Lead DevOps — excluded from corpus.
- Panel: R_1, R_2 (recruiters), P_1, P_2 (MEng peers at Imperial). Separate from
  the Study 14/15 HCI panel.
- 12 sessions total (4 raters × 3 roles). People read the JD + fixed skill list
  with TF-IDF hidden. Scale: 1 = essential, 5 = optional (same as the UI).
- Consensus = median of the four integer ratings per skill on that role.
- Compared to round(6 - suggested_weight) from the offline TF-IDF recompute.

n = 27 skill–role pairs (not 108 individual votes). Each dot on the scatter is
one skill on one job, Y = that median, X = TF-IDF mapped to the same scale.

Headline numbers (from run_study.py / evaluation_report.json)
-------------------------------------------------------------
Aggregate (27 pairs):
  - Exact match: 29.6%  (~3 in 10 land on the same 1–5 band)
  - MAE: 1.26           (usually off by about one full priority step)
  - Spearman rho: 0.25, p ≈ 0.20  (weak pooled ordering; not sig at 0.05)

Per hold-out:
  - Frontend:  9 skills, 44% exact, MAE 0.78, rho 0.52
  - ML:        8 skills, 25% exact, MAE 1.88, rho -0.45  ← worst role
  - DevOps:   10 skills, 20% exact, MAE 1.20, rho 0.40

Exact agreements (8 pairs) — both sides hit the same band:
  React, HTML, CSS, Git (FE); SQL, PostgreSQL (ML); Linux, CS degree (DevOps).

Backend suggested_weight band is [1.0, 5.0] (min-max per JD); UI uses round(6 - weight).

Biggest mismatches (worth showing in the report):
  - ML PhD/MSc education line: humans P-5 (all four agreed — not a filter),
    TF-IDF P-1. Gap of 4. Rare token in corpus ≠ important to shortlist.
  - ML Python: humans P-1, TF-IDF P-5 (backend 1.0 on that JD). Worst-case mismatch after [1.0,5.0] scale.
  - ML CUDA: humans ~P-3–4, TF-IDF P-1. Inverted emphasis.
  - FE hygiene (HTML, TypeScript, etc.): humans P-4/P-5, TF-IDF often P-3/P-4.
    Stuff in the JD text gets mid-tier weights even when recruiters treat it as noise.

Qualitative stuff that mattered
---------------------------------
Recruiters basically live at P-1/P-2 or P-4/P-5; R_1 said they rarely touch P-3 on
junior FE ("must-have or noise"). Peers sometimes nudged hygiene skills one step
higher but agreed on the headline stack.

The long "PhD or MSc in Machine Learning..." row is the JD education sentence copied
verbatim into the Education metric — not a normalised skill. Everyone scored it P-5
after we aligned the pilot narrative (credential line ≠ role fit). TF-IDF still
loves it because it's rare in the corpus. That's the clearest "don't auto-default
this" example in the study.

Scale-use asymmetry (worth stating in the thesis)
-------------------------------------------------
TF-IDF min-max on each JD *forces* a spread: something maps to backend 1.0 and something to 5.0 (→ UI P-5 and P-1).
Humans don't have that rule — they can pile many skills at P-4/P-5 (hygiene) without TF-IDF's artificial contrast.
Recruiters rarely used P-3; lots of polarisation. Comparing MAE/Spearman across two different uses of the same 1–5 scale
is part of why exact match looks low even when qualitatively both sides know what's headline vs noise.

Plain-English conclusion
------------------------
TF-IDF in MERIT is useful as a *hint* in some contexts (DevOps toolchain ranked
reasonably; a handful of exact matches), but it's not safe as silent 1–5 defaults.

Calibration is poor overall (low exact match, MAE ~1.2). Ordering is mixed — good
on tight infra JDs, actively wrong on senior ML when Python/PyTorch matter most.

That matches the product choice: show suggestions, let recruiters override, especially
for common languages, web glue, and degree boilerplate.

Caveats we're honest about in the thesis
----------------------------------------
- Pilot only: 4 people, 3 synthetic hold-outs, facilitator-structured ratings in
  populate_ground_truth.py (session notes are real themes; numbers are archived
  for reproducibility, not a large independent panel study).
- Fixed skill lists — doesn't include parser misses from Study 04.
- Synthetic corpus (tonic.ai batch generation): we didn't scrape real job boards.
  Tonic lets you steer batch JD synthesis (role mix, templates), so IDF partly
  measures "what we generated" not a neutral market. Oversampling certain stacks
  would change what's "rare" in IDF.
- Time-specific snapshot: IDF is a picture of 2025–26 corpus + hold-outs. Python
  looks common → TF-IDF down-ranks it on ML even when recruiters say P-1. That
  could flip in a future labour market when new stacks dominate; production would
  need corpus refresh/versioning.
- Pooled Spearman across all skills/roles is exploratory; role-level rho is more
  interpretable.

Hypotheses (how we read them after the run)
-------------------------------------------
H1 (ordinal alignment): partial — positive pooled rho but p > 0.05; DevOps saves it.
H2 (low MAE / good calibration): not met — ~22% exact match speaks for itself.

Future work if we had more time: run on parser-extracted skills, real corpus slice,
role-aware IDF, don't run education strings through the same TF-IDF path as Python.
